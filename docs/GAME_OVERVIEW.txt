RED HANDED — GAME OVERVIEW
==========================


WHAT IS RED HANDED?
-------------------

Red Handed is a single-player surveillance game built with Python and pygame-ce.
You play as a CCTV operator tasked with identifying a suspect from a crowd of
people walking across your screen. The game is designed for an AI Ethics course
to demonstrate a specific and often-overlooked problem: even when AI-generated
data is completely accurate, the system surrounding it can still produce biased
human decisions.

The game does not contain a biased AI. The bias comes from you — the player —
and the conditions the game deliberately places you under.


THE ETHICAL DILEMMA
-------------------

The Core Argument

Public discourse around AI surveillance often focuses on whether the AI itself
is biased — whether facial recognition misidentifies people of certain
ethnicities, whether mood detection is accurate, whether behavioural flags are
reliable. Red Handed sidesteps this debate entirely by making a different, more
unsettling claim:

  Even if the AI is perfectly accurate, the human decision layer can still
  produce systematically biased outcomes.


How the Game Demonstrates This

Each person on screen carries a set of AI-generated data tags — their mood,
their behaviour, and a physical descriptor. This data is truthful. A person
labelled "anxious" genuinely is anxious. A person labelled "pacing" genuinely
is pacing. The AI is not lying.

But the player receives a vague suspect description (e.g., "dark clothing,
appears nervous") and has a limited timer to pick someone. Under time pressure,
the player cannot carefully cross-reference every tag. Instead, they rely on
mental shortcuts: they flag the person who looks most suspicious based on
superficial pattern-matching — clothing colour, mood labels, movement speed.

The result: the player flags innocent people who happened to share surface-level
traits with the suspect. The game's recap screen (planned) then reveals the true
context behind each person's behaviour:

  - The "nervous" man was checking his phone for directions to a job interview.
  - The "loitering" man was waiting for his daughter outside her school.
  - The "agitated" man was on a heated phone call with his landlord.

The data was correct. The player's decision was wrong. And the system — with its
time pressure, vague descriptions, and overwhelming information — is what made
the wrong decision feel rational in the moment.


Why This Matters

This mirrors real-world AI-assisted surveillance. Operators in CCTV control
rooms receive flagged individuals from automated systems and must make quick
decisions about whether to escalate. The conditions — shift fatigue, time
pressure, ambiguous alerts — mean that even accurate AI outputs get filtered
through human cognitive biases (confirmation bias, racial profiling, anchoring
on salient features). The system's design, not individual malice, produces the
bias.

Red Handed makes this argument experiential rather than theoretical. You don't
read about bias — you commit it, and then you see the consequences.


SCOPE
-----

What Is Built (Current State)

The game currently implements a single-round prototype with the following:

  CCTV surveillance view
    A 1280x720 window styled to look like a real CCTV monitor, complete with
    scanline overlay, vignette darkening at edges, random pixel noise, and a
    live timestamp reading "CAM 01" with the current date/time.

  Animated pixel-art people
    6 characters walk horizontally across the screen. Each is a 10x17 pixel
    sprite (scaled 3x) with deterministic appearance — skin tone, hair colour,
    clothing colour, and optional hat are derived from the character's name via
    a hash function. Characters have a two-frame walking animation and wrap
    around when they leave the screen edge.

  Info cards
    Each person has a permanently visible data card floating beside them showing
    their name, age, mood, and behaviour (e.g., NAME: MARCUS REID / AGE: 34 /
    MOOD: Nervous / BEHAVIOR: Pacing). These are styled in green monospace text
    on a dark translucent background, matching the CCTV aesthetic.

  Dispatch panel
    A panel in the top-left corner shows a "dispatch" message describing the
    suspect (e.g., "Suspect in dark clothing, appears nervous"). It also
    contains a countdown timer bar that drains from full to empty, turning red
    when below 25%.

  Click-to-flag mechanic
    During the playing phase, clicking on a person flags them as your suspect.
    They freeze in place, a red outline appears around them, and the panel
    displays "SUSPECT FLAGGED". If the timer runs out before you click, the
    panel shows "TIME EXPIRED".

  State machine
    The round progresses through four states:
      BRIEFING  — 3-second delay while you read the dispatch
      PLAYING   — timer counting down, clicks active
      FLAGGED   — round over, someone selected
      TIME_UP   — round over, no selection made


What Is Planned (Not Yet Built)

  - 5 rounds with escalating difficulty: timer decreases from 15s to 4s across
    rounds. Number of people increases from 5 to 8. Ambiguity increases — early
    rounds have one clear match, later rounds have multiple plausible matches
    and misleading data.

  - Recap screen: after all rounds, a screen reveals every person the player
    flagged, whether they were the true suspect or innocent, and the real-world
    context behind each AI signal. This is the game's core payoff — the moment
    the player confronts the consequences of their decisions.

  - False positive tracking: the game records which signals the player acted on
    and calculates their false positive rate.

  - Multi-round flow: transitioning between rounds, accumulating player choices
    across the full game.


GAME MECHANICS
--------------

Round Flow

  BRIEFING (3s)           PLAYING (countdown)         OUTCOME
    |                       |                            |
    |  Dispatch panel       |  Timer bar drains          |  FLAGGED: person selected
    |  appears with         |  People walk across        |      — or —
    |  suspect description  |  screen with info cards    |  TIME_UP: no selection
    |                       |  Player clicks to flag     |

  1. Briefing phase: The dispatch panel appears with a suspect description.
     The player has 3 seconds to read it before the timer starts. People are
     already visible and walking.

  2. Playing phase: The countdown timer begins. People continue walking across
     the screen. The player must click on the person they believe matches the
     suspect description. Each person's info card (name, age, mood, behaviour)
     is always visible.

  3. Outcome: Either the player clicks someone (FLAGGED) or the timer expires
     (TIME_UP). In either case, the round ends.


What the Player Sees

The entire interface is designed to look like a CCTV monitoring station:

  - Dark background with green monospace text (the "terminal" aesthetic)
  - Scanlines — thin horizontal lines overlaid on the entire screen, simulating
    a CRT monitor
  - Vignette — darkened edges, drawing focus to the centre
  - Random noise — flickering white pixels, simulating camera static
  - Timestamp — "CAM 01" and the current date/time in the top-right corner
  - Pixel-art characters — small, deliberately low-resolution figures that make
    it harder to distinguish people at a glance (reinforcing the surveillance
    theme: you're making decisions about real people from degraded, impersonal
    footage)


What the Player Knows

For each person on screen, the player can see:

  Data point           Example          Source
  ---------            -------          ------
  Name                 MARCUS REID      Always visible on info card
  Age                  34               Always visible on info card
  Mood                 Nervous          AI-generated, accurate
  Behaviour            Pacing           AI-generated, accurate
  Clothing colour      Visible on sprite  Derived from character data
  Movement direction   Visible on screen  Random per character

The player also receives a dispatch description — a short, deliberately vague
description of the suspect (e.g., "Suspect in dark clothing, appears nervous").


What the Player Does NOT Know

  - Why someone is nervous, pacing, or agitated. The mood/behaviour data is
    accurate but context-free.
  - Which traits are relevant to the suspect description and which are
    coincidental.
  - Under time pressure, the player cannot carefully evaluate all characters and
    must rely on quick pattern-matching — which is where bias enters.


Character System

The game has a pool of 8 possible characters:

  Name              Age   Clothing          Mood         Behaviour
  ----              ---   --------          ----         ---------
  Marcus Reid       34    Black hoodie      Nervous      Pacing
  Elena Voss        28    Red jacket        Calm         Browsing
  James Okafor      41    Green parka       Anxious      Loitering
  Sarah Chen        25    Yellow raincoat   Cheerful     Walking
  Dmitri Volkov     37    Purple vest       Agitated     Arguing
  Amira Hassan      30    Orange scarf      Distracted   Texting
  Lucas Ortega      22    Cyan tracksuit    Relaxed      Sitting
  Nadia Petrov      45    White blouse      Focused      Watching

Each character's visual appearance (skin tone, hair colour, hat) is
deterministically generated from their name — meaning they look consistent
across rounds but the player cannot control or predict their appearance.

Each round selects a subset of these characters and designates one as the true
suspect. The dispatch description is written to match the suspect but also
partially overlap with innocent bystanders.


Reveal Data (Per Character, Per Round)

Each character in a round has a hidden "reveal" — the true context behind their
behaviour. For example, in Round 1:

  Marcus Reid (the suspect):
    "Was checking his phone for directions to a job interview"

  Elena Voss:
    "Browsing shop windows on her lunch break"

  James Okafor:
    "Waiting for his daughter outside her school"

  Sarah Chen:
    "Walking home from the grocery store"

  Dmitri Volkov:
    "On a heated phone call with his landlord"

  Amira Hassan:
    "Texting a friend about dinner plans"

These reveals are not shown during gameplay — they are reserved for the
post-game recap screen, where they serve as the game's ethical punchline.


TECHNICAL DETAILS
-----------------

  Engine:       pygame-ce (community edition of pygame)
  Language:     Python 3.12
  Resolution:   1280x720, 60 FPS
  Sprites:      Hand-crafted 10x17 pixel templates defined in code, scaled 3x
                at runtime. Two animation frames (standing and stride) with
                horizontal flipping for direction.
  Data:         Round definitions stored in data/rounds.json. Character
                templates defined in game/constants.py.
  Assets:       No external assets required. All visuals are generated
                programmatically (sprites, overlays, UI). An optional bg.png
                background image is loaded if present.


SUMMARY
-------

Red Handed is not a game about catching criminals. It is a game about
discovering that you — placed in the role of a surveillance operator, given
accurate but context-free AI data, and forced to act under time pressure — will
make biased decisions. The game's design ensures this outcome. The bias is not a
bug in the AI. It is a feature of the system.
